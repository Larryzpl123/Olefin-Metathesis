Introduction

Definition & scope

Olefin metathesis is a catalytic reaction in which two alkenes exchange the substituents attached to their carbon–carbon double bonds. In its most general representation, two R¹HC=CHR² and R³HC=CHR⁴ substrates are converted into R¹HC=CHR³ and R²HC=CHR⁴ (plus the statistical mix of other combinations). The reaction is mediated by a transition-metal alkylidene — a compound in which a metal center is doubly bonded to a carbon atom — and it proceeds through a strained four-membered metallacyclobutane intermediate.2

Because the C=C bond is a structural element of enormous generality — it appears in pharmaceuticals, polymers, fragrances, natural products, and commodity petrochemicals — a reaction that rearranges it with broad tolerance for other functional groups has applications across nearly all of synthetic chemistry. Metathesis does exactly this. It is reversible, typically run near equilibrium, and driven forward either by release of a volatile byproduct (most often ethylene) or by relief of ring strain.

Etymology

The term metathesis comes from the Greek μετάθεσις ("transposition"), describing the exchange of bonded partners. In grammar, it denotes the rearrangement of sounds within a word; in chemistry, it denotes the rearrangement of atoms across two molecules.

This monograph treats the four principal modes of olefin metathesis — cross metathesis (CM), ring-closing metathesis (RCM), ring-opening metathesis (ROM), and ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) — along with two important variants (ADMET and enyne metathesis). For each, the driving force, typical catalyst, and selectivity considerations are discussed.

Historical Development

From mystery reaction to mechanism

The phenomenon predates the name. In the 1950s, chemists at DuPont, Phillips Petroleum, and Standard Oil observed that certain heterogeneous catalysts could convert propylene into a mixture of ethylene and 2-butenes. No one knew what was happening. The transformation was formally a "disproportionation," and it was initially interpreted through the lens of then-familiar radical or ionic mechanisms, none of which fit the experimental facts.3

In 1971, Yves Chauvin and his student Jean-Louis Hérisson, working at the Institut Français du Pétrole, proposed the correct mechanism: a catalytic cycle involving a metal carbene (alkylidene) that reacts with an alkene to form a metallacyclobutane, which then cleaves in the opposite sense to regenerate a new metal carbene and release a new alkene.4 The proposal was elegant, but heterodox — no well-defined metal alkylidene was known to catalyze the reaction at the time.

The validation of Chauvin's mechanism required the discovery of well-defined, isolable catalysts that behaved as the mechanism predicted. Richard Schrock at MIT developed the first of these: highly active molybdenum and tungsten alkylidene complexes that were sensitive to moisture and oxygen but extraordinarily effective.5 Robert Grubbs at Caltech then developed a family of ruthenium-based catalysts — less active than Schrock's, but much more tolerant of functional groups, moisture, and air.6 The Grubbs catalysts transformed metathesis from a specialist tool into a reaction any synthetic chemist could run on the benchtop.

The 2005 Prize was about finding something useful: a catalyst that works for almost any alkene, in almost any solvent, in the presence of almost any functional group. paraphrased, Royal Swedish Academy press release, 2005

The Chauvin Mechanism

Catalytic cycle via metallacyclobutane

Chauvin's mechanism is a four-step catalytic cycle. A metal alkylidene [M]=CHR binds an incoming alkene, undergoes a formal [2+2] cycloaddition to give a metallacyclobutane, and then fragments in the reverse (retro-[2+2]) sense to release a new alkene and a new metal alkylidene. The catalyst identity is preserved through the cycle, but the alkylidene substituent exchanges with each turnover.

Productive vs. Non-productive Cycles

The metallacyclobutane can fragment in two ways: along the "original" diagonal (regenerating the starting materials — a degenerate or non-productive cycle) or along the other diagonal (generating the product — a productive cycle). For disubstituted metallacyclobutanes, both outcomes are possible, and the ratio depends on the steric and electronic environment at the metal. This is why metathesis is intrinsically reversible: every productive turnover is matched by an unproductive (or reverse-productive) alternative.

The Dissociative Pathway (Grubbs Catalysts)

For Grubbs-type ruthenium catalysts, the resting state is a 16-electron complex of the form (L)(PCy₃)Cl₂Ru=CHPh. Before metathesis can occur, a phosphine (or, for second-generation catalysts, the NHC partner) must dissociate to generate the active 14-electron species. Substrate alkene then binds in the vacated coordination site, and the [2+2]/retro-[2+2] cycle proceeds. The rate of phosphine dissociation is therefore rate-determining for most Grubbs-catalyzed metathesis.7

Catalysts

Schrock, Grubbs, Hoveyda

Modern metathesis practice is defined by a small set of well-characterized catalysts. They differ primarily in the metal (Mo, W, or Ru), the supporting ligands (alkoxides, imido, phosphines, N-heterocyclic carbenes), and the identity of the initiating alkylidene. Each offers a different balance of activity, functional-group tolerance, and stereoselectivity.

Schrock Mo/W catalysts
Mo imido alkoxide · 1990

Extremely active — often the only catalysts capable of metathesizing tri- and tetrasubstituted alkenes. Intolerant of protic and polar functional groups, moisture, and air. Typically prepared and handled in a glovebox.

A loose rule of thumb: Schrock catalysts for the hardest substrates (under inert atmosphere); Grubbs II or Hoveyda–Grubbs II for nearly everything else; Grubbs III when initiation speed matters. For comprehensive discussions of catalyst design and choice, see the two-volume Handbook of Metathesis.8

Reaction Types

CM · RCM · ROM · ROMP · ADMET · Enyne

Metathesis is classified by the topology of the substrate(s) and the product(s). Six modes account for the vast majority of applications.

CM Cross Metathesis
two acyclic alkenes → two new acyclic alkenes

Two acyclic alkenes are combined to give a new acyclic alkene (plus, typically, ethylene as the byproduct if terminal alkenes are used). CM is the simplest metathesis transformation in principle but the most selectivity-sensitive in practice — a mixture of A+B substrates can give AA, AB, and BB products statistically unless one or both partners is "typed" for selective cross-coupling. Grubbs introduced a four-category classification of alkenes (Types I–IV) based on how readily they homodimerize, which remains the standard framework for predicting CM outcomes.9

RCM Ring-Closing Metathesis
α,ω-diene → cycloalkene + ethylene

An acyclic α,ω-diene undergoes intramolecular metathesis to give a cyclic alkene, extruding ethylene. RCM is the most synthetically prized of all metathesis modes — it constructs rings of almost any size (5-, 6-, 7-membered are routine; macrocycles are common) under mild conditions, with broad functional-group tolerance. It is now a standard disconnection for macrocyclic drug candidates.

ROM Ring-Opening Metathesis
strained cycloalkene + alkene → acyclic diene

A strained cyclic alkene (typically norbornene or cyclobutene) is opened by a second alkene to give an acyclic product containing both new alkene units. ROM is the single-turnover cousin of ROMP: if the ring-opened alkylidene is terminated before it can react with another cyclic monomer, the isolated product is a discrete molecule rather than a polymer. Driven by release of ~27 kcal mol⁻¹ of strain for norbornene.

ROMP Ring-Opening Metathesis Polymerization
strained cycloalkene → polymer with one alkene per repeat unit

When a strained cyclic alkene reacts with a metal alkylidene, the resulting ring-opened alkylidene can react with another monomer, then another, and so on — each turnover adding one monomer to a growing chain. The product is a polymer containing exactly one C=C double bond per repeat unit. ROMP is the method of choice for well-defined polynorbornenes, polycyclooctenes (→ polyoctenamer rubber, marketed as Vestenamer), and numerous dicyclopentadiene-based thermosets (e.g., reaction injection molded parts). Living ROMP with Grubbs III gives polymers with narrow molecular-weight distributions (Đ ≈ 1.05) and enables precise block copolymer synthesis.10

ADMET Acyclic Diene Metathesis Polymerization
α,ω-diene monomers → unsaturated polymer + ethylene

An α,ω-diene can either close (RCM) or polymerize head-to-tail (ADMET). Which one wins depends on concentration, ring size accessibility, and catalyst: dilute conditions favor RCM; bulk or concentrated conditions favor ADMET. The reaction is a step-growth polymerization driven by ethylene loss, and it produces strictly linear polymers with one internal alkene per repeat unit.

Enyne Enyne Metathesis
alkene + alkyne → 1,3-diene

An alkene and an alkyne combine to give a 1,3-diene. Unlike the other metathesis modes, enyne metathesis is atom-economical — no small-molecule byproduct is released, because the alkyne supplies two carbons that are incorporated into the diene. The mechanism involves a vinyl carbene intermediate; the intramolecular version (ring-closing enyne metathesis, RCEYM) is a particularly powerful way to construct cyclic 1,3-dienes that can be elaborated by Diels–Alder chemistry.11

Selectivity & Thermodynamics

E/Z ratios, equilibrium, driving force

Because every step in the Chauvin cycle is reversible, metathesis reactions reach a thermodynamic mixture unless something is done to push them forward. Four strategies are commonly used:

1. Ethylene extrusion

For reactions that produce ethylene (CM of terminal alkenes, RCM, ADMET), running open to the atmosphere or under partial vacuum removes ethylene and shifts the equilibrium toward products. For RCM of macrocycles, sparging with nitrogen or argon is standard.

2. Ring strain release

For ROM and ROMP, the driving force is relief of ring strain in the cyclic substrate. Norbornene (≈ 27 kcal mol⁻¹ strain) and cyclobutene (≈ 30 kcal mol⁻¹) are excellent ROMP monomers; cyclopentene (~ 6 kcal mol⁻¹) polymerizes only under high monomer concentrations; cyclohexene does not ROMP at all — it is unstrained, and its polymer, polybutadienylene, is higher in energy than the monomer.

3. Precipitation

For polymerizations, precipitation of the insoluble polymer product removes it from equilibrium and drives the reaction.

4. Stereoselective catalyst design

With most Ru catalysts, the thermodynamic E:Z ratio in internal alkenes is typically 3:1 to 5:1 in favor of E (trans). Achieving selectivity for the Z (cis) isomer requires specially designed catalysts. Schrock, Hoveyda, and Grubbs have each developed Z-selective catalyst families; these are now mature enough for use in the synthesis of cis-double-bond-containing natural products such as prostaglandins and insect pheromones.12

Caveat

Chemoselectivity depends strongly on catalyst identity. Grubbs I tolerates alcohols, aldehydes, amides, carboxylic acids, and water; it does not tolerate free amines, thiols, or phosphines (all of which coordinate ruthenium). Grubbs II extends the tolerance to many electron-poor alkenes (e.g., acrylates). Schrock catalysts tolerate none of these in any useful way.

Applications

Medicine, polymers, commodity chemicals

Metathesis has moved from curiosity to indispensable tool across scales, from laboratory total synthesis to multi-kiloton industrial polymer production.

Pharmaceutical synthesis

RCM is routine for constructing macrocycles and medium-ring heterocycles in drug candidates.

e.g. simeprevir, vaniprevir (HCV NS3/4A inhibitors)
Polymer manufacturing

ROMP produces commodity and specialty polymers with exactly one C=C per repeat unit.

e.g. Norsorex, Vestenamer, DCPD thermosets
Petrochemicals

Shell's SHOP process and OCT (olefin conversion technology) produce linear α-olefins at megaton scale via metathesis.

e.g. propylene from ethylene + 2-butene
Natural product synthesis

Macrolides, cyclopeptides, and terpenoid skeletons — many built by RCM in their disconnection.

e.g. epothilones, platensimycin, coibacin A
Oleochemistry / biodiesel

Cross metathesis of methyl oleate with ethylene produces 1-decene and methyl 9-decenoate — renewable α-olefins from seed oils.

e.g. Elevance Renewable Sciences process
Materials / surfaces

Surface-initiated ROMP decorates surfaces with polymer brushes of controlled thickness and composition.

e.g. antifouling coatings, sensor interfaces

The breadth of application reflects the unusual fit of metathesis to modern synthesis: high selectivity for C=C in the presence of heteroatoms; mild conditions (often room temperature); tolerance of water and air for Ru catalysts; scalability from milligram to metric-ton; and an intuitive, retrosynthetically useful disconnection (every internal alkene becomes a potential metathesis disconnection).
